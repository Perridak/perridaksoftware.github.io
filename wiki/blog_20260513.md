# My Personal, Self-Inflicted, AI Hell
After setting up a brand spanking new business, I thought it was time to learn a new platform and language that I believe will be best aligned to help me achieve my business goals. And since nothing comes without risk, what better way to begin than to bet both my business and my approaching retirement on this. I've settled on Dart & Flutter, as this will help me to achieve a visual consistency in the apps that I make cross-platform, and on the surface, it doesn't seem to be too difficult to pick up relatively quickly.

Naturally, I have also set myself a stupidly unreasonable deadline for my first product release, like, I've become my own pointy-haired boss, and I can't get away with padding my estimates this time... What was I thinking!!!

Given my tight schedule, I want to learn the language and gain an element of proficiency fast.  Trolling through the many amazing resources available for free online is too slow for my tastes, so I thought I'd build a kind of tutorial summary of everything that I will need to get up to speed quickly. At the same time, I'd indulge my curiosity and explore this sensation that is causing stupid bosses to fire whole software engineering departments on the dubious premise that "AI can do it as well, if not better than the average software developer can".

I played around with Claude AI for a bit because it seemed to behave a little better than many other offerings.  While I find its responses to be pandering and overly insincere at times, it seemed to do a good job at doing some of the research tasks I set for it to do, and I didn't like how it would stop mid-job when my free allocation had run out.  Yes, there are plenty of other free systems out there, but something drew me to Claude that made me feel I could work well with it.

So far, so good. Solid research can be a pain at the best of times, and I quickly learned that I could use Claude like an overly complex search engine, but doing what I want, not pedantically what I type, and intuitively finding better keywords than I could come up with in twice the time. Add to that having no ads or placement algorithms to deal with, and it was instantly worth the fee for a year so that I could really put it through its paces. I also picked up the trick that I could get it to remember key things that we found in the search, so that I could circle back or keep a running list of things I wanted to remember.  I usually name it my "backburner" list, and I have a setting that Claude should always end a response with my backburner items and the date and time, so I can track it all later.

Connecting the dots between my newfound AI "obsession" and my desire to build a tutorial aid quickly, I thought I'd test the boundaries of this tool and my relationship with it, and get it to build a tutorial website, complete with a simple quiz and a bunch of chapters, summarising the best of what we found online for learning Dart and Flutter, and seeing just how good a programmer it could be with an admittedly limited spec and a small project. It's the sort of task you throw at a junior or an intern expecting them to take too long and mess it up a bit, and that you could do yourself in about a day if you have all of the content available... aaand down the rabbit-hole I went.

Ask Claude to analyse a logo for a colour palette and get it to build a simple HTML/CSS thing, and it's brilliant.  Definitely a time saver there. Ask it to do anything even remotely requiring a little thought, and it will try to push a simple single web page design onto a server, embed acres of JavaScript, and tell you that something as simple as loading one page into another can't be done locally. Even when you tell it EXACTLY what you want, it struggles to do it, and you will find yourself quickly getting frustrated with a software tool that is wholly unsatisfying to swear at.  Don't worry, I'm my own HR department as well, so I'll give myself a good talking to later about workplace culture and respect... I.. er... promise!

I spent 3 days trying to get Claude to do what I wanted.  I had started out a little vague, and Claude just tried to fill in the gaps without verification. It just did its own thing, which was really concerning, and is the key reason why I will never allow an AI into my file system, git repositories, or able to edit anything outside the confines of the AI itself.  It doesn't know any better, and if you aren't extremely precise with how you instruct it. I'm talking about the kind of fine-grained planning that makes it generally easier to just jump in and do the job yourself.  its saving grace is that you can define some rules for it to follow, and I learned that I could put certain phrases together to keep it mostly on track.  By the end of day 2, I had my rules of engagement clearly defined, based on frustrations earned over the previous couple of days, and I restarted the tutorial project to see what would change and whether I could turn Claude's weakness into an advantage.  

The new approach worked well, for the most part. I spent 4 or 5 very productive hours, and got Claude to build a nice scaffold for the web page, generate some rudimentary content, lay out the chapters, and came up with a process to make editing the pages really easy using markdown-formatted text, and parsing the files to generate the chapter content for my tutorial. It's an elegant design, makes use of JavaScript but relies mostly on HTML/CSS, is a very responsive page, works nicely on mobiles as well as in very small windows, and does its own syntax highlighting because the existing APIs didn't really support Dart all too well or were incomplete for the language. I really thought I had cracked the problem, and that my career as a software developer could actually be at risk... until Claud did what all AIs do, it started to hallucinate big time, and argue with me that a simple thing that I wanted done could not be done, even when I told it step by step, and function by function how to do it.  Even when I told it to stop telling me the same stuff over and over again, and to go online and check its sources, it argued like that annoying person who knows it all, and will argue till they're blue in the face, even when everyone else is proving to them how wrong they are! 

I got the project mostly completed. I just need to source the tutorial material, and I'll be able to use it to learn the ins and outs of Dart and Flutter. The whole project took me almost as long as it will take me to learn the new language and APIs, but I didn't entirely waste my time, and I learned a lot of valuable lessons along the way. I still have a useful resource that I will make available for free online because, while technically the work is still mine, it doesn't feel right charging anyone for AI generated stuff... and while I think that the end result has turned out quite well, It isn't to the standards that I set for myself and my business, and I know I would have done it better myself without the AI.

So what did I learn?
1. It's scarily awesome for a while, until it isn't.
1. You have to have a list of very precise instructions at the start, that clearly define the rules about how you will work and what you expect of the AI. Like, literally tell it not to edit anything without checking with you first.
1. AIs are usually poorly designed on the UI side of things.  
   * Web browsers struggle when you have too much data in the page, and AI conversations can get really long, really fast. 
   * I see the UI as a serious design flaw that affects other AIs as well, such as Co-pilot and Gemini, for example. Even in their dedicated apps with less of the browser overhead, they get really janky when a single chat gets too long.
   * Most AI's keep your chats on the server so you can use them on multiple devices, yet they still preload an entire chat, rather than paging just enough to keep up with scrolling through them and leaving the rest on the server where it belongs.
1. Web browser performance bottlenecks due to long chats can cause hallucinations just the same as old data can. 
1. Use an AI for a while, then save what you've done at a logical point, start a new chat, upload your files, and continue. It's better than starting over, and it helps to keep your system performance from lagging behind like a kid trying to get out of taking a bath.
1. Even if you have the best prompts and AI knowledge, you will run into issues.  Telling the AI not to echo every thought it is having helps minimise the memory usage, but it still slows down eventually, and the output of the AI can't be relied on as the objective truth.
1. It's hard work to use well as a software developer.  It will be even harder for someone without software development knowledge to make the AI create or maintain a code-base, so if your job isn't safe, be prepared to be hired back as a contractor for tons more money than you got before they fired you. I'm serious, if you are a business exec thinking about replacing staff with AI, don't. It's decades away from being able to do any job well without the worst micromanagement you could possibly imagine.

For pure research, AI is the best thing ever, and I will continue to use it carefully, but will always check its sources and read them for myself. For software development, I can do it better myself, and I really don't need the "help".
